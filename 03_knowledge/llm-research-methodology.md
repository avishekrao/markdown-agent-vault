# Research methodology using LLM

## Essence
Systematized best practices for conducting research using large language models: how to get high-quality, relevant results, what distortions to expect and how to compensate for them.

## Details

### Fundamental Limitations of the LLM as a Research Tool

**1. Hallucinations**
28% of LLM outputs contain plausible but factually incorrect information. There are: entity hallucinations (errors in names, companies, dates, API versions), attribution hallucinations (a fact that is true, but attributed to the wrong source), intrinsic (internal inconsistency of the answer) and extrinsic (contradiction with external facts).

**2. Instability of results**The results vary at 36.4% of repeat requests. The same question may yield different answers in different sessions. This is not a bug - it is a property of stochastic generation.

**3. Training data bias**
LLMs reproduce and amplify distortions from training data. For commercial topics, this is especially critical: training data contains a disproportionate amount of content marketing, SEO articles and advertising materials. The model “thinks” the way the Internet “thinks”—and the Internet disproportionately represents the voices of those who sell.

**4. Position bias**
When evaluating multiple options, LLM systematically favors the first or last option. GPT-4 shows up to 40% inconsistency depending on the order of presentation.

**5. Verbosity bias**
LLM considers longer, more detailed responses to be of higher quality. A more verbose answer receives a ~15% overscore.

**6. Self-enhancement bias**
Models prefer content generated by other LLMs, with an overestimation of 5-7%. AI prefers AI - this is confirmed by the PNAS 2025 study.

### Distortion map for LLM research

#### Vocal Minority Bias**Problem:** When researching any commercial topic, LLMs (and search engines) return primarily the opinions of three groups:
- **Sellers** are companies that promote their product through content marketing. Their articles are SEO-optimized and rank at the top of search results.
- **Screamers** (vocal minority) - people with strong emotions (negative or positive) who write reviews and posts. The silent majority is not represented.
- **Evangelists** are enthusiasts who promote certain approaches/tools out of belief.

Research by MIT Sloan and ScienceDaily (2025) confirms that a small group of active users creates most of the content, people overestimate the degree of toxicity and polarization of the Internet, and opinions in social media are systematically biased by the vocal minority.

**Compensation:**
- Explicitly ask for alternative points of view: “What are the arguments AGAINST this approach?”
- Seek sources from academic/independent research rather than blogs
- Separately ask: “Which of those answering this question has a commercial interest?”
- Ask the “silent majority”: “What does a typical user who doesn’t post do?”

#### Survivorship Bias**Problem:** LLM knows about successful companies, products, approaches - but does not know about those who tried the same thing and failed. There are few post-mortems and many success stories in training data.

**Compensation:**
- Explicitly ask: “Which companies/projects have UNSUCCESSFULLY tried this approach? Why?"
- “What are the typical reasons for failure when using [X]?”
- Separately search for postmortems and failure reports

####Authority Bias

**Problem:** LLM overestimates the opinions of large companies (FAANG, McKinsey) and underestimates the practices of small businesses, niche players, emerging markets.

**Compensation:**
- “How is this problem solved in companies of up to 50 people / in the CIS markets / in startups?”
- “Is there a contrary opinion from practitioners rather than consultants?”

#### Recency Bias / Outdated Knowledge

**Problem:** LLM may produce outdated information, especially for rapidly changing areas (SaaS tools, APIs, regulations). Or, conversely, overestimate the latest trends.

**Compensation:**
- Always clarify: “Is this relevant as of [current date]?”
- Use web search for verification
- Ask about evolution: “How has this approach changed over the past 3 years?”#### Content marketing noise (Commercial Content Noise)

**Problem:** For commercial queries, LLM inevitably incorporates knowledge from content marketing articles that promote specific products/approaches.

**Compensation:**
- Ask for principles, not tools: “What are the underlying PRINCIPLES?” instead of “What is the best tool?”
- Ask for classification: “Divide the answer into: (1) facts, (2) opinions, (3) advertising claims.”
- Ask disclaimer: “Please check if any statement may come from a commercial source.”
- Use negative prompting: “Do not recommend specific commercial products, describe the selection criteria”

### Practical techniques for quality LLM research

#### Technique 1: Triangulation

Concept from scientific methodology: use at least 2-3 independent methods to study one question. For LLM research:

- **Between models:** ask one question to several LLMs (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini). If the answers differ, this is an area of ​​uncertainty. Research shows that using 3-5 majority vote models reduces distortion by 30-40%.
- **Between modalities:** LLM answer + web search + academic sources.- **Between roles:** ask the same LLM to answer on behalf of different experts: “Answer as a freelance practitioner,” “Answer as a researcher,” “Answer as a skeptic.”

#### Technique 2: Chain of Verification (CoV)

Principle: After generating the response, LLM checks each statement. Practical implementation:

1. Get the basic answer
2. Ask: “List all the factual statements in this answer.”
3. For each statement: “How confident are you? What source?
4. Separately check the most critical statements via web search

#### Technique 3: Adversarial Prompting

After receiving a response:
- “What is the strongest argument AGAINST this conclusion?”
- “Imagine that you are a critic - what are the holes in this analysis?”
- “What am I missing? What are the blind spots?
- “If I showed this conclusion to [a skeptical expert], what would he say?”

#### Technique 4: Decomposition (decomposition of complex queries)

Break a large research question into 5-7 sub-questions and investigate each separately. It reduces hallucinations because:
- The model focuses on a narrow context
- It is easier to verify each sub-answer
- Less temptation for the model to “fill in the gaps” with fiction#### Technique 5: Source-First Research (from sources, not from conclusions)

Instead of “Tell about X” → “Find 5 primary sources (not blogs) on topic X, briefly describe each and provide key figures/conclusions.”

This makes the model work from facts rather than narrative.

#### Technique 6: Explicitly marking confidence

In the prompt: “For each statement, indicate the degree of confidence: ✅ confirmed by many sources / ⚠️ found in several sources, but controversial / ❓ my guess based on patterns / 🔴 cannot confirm.”

This forces the model to be reflective instead of confidently generating.

#### Technique 7: Red Team prompt

A separate step after the research: “You are the red team. Your task: to find all errors, inaccuracies, unsubstantiated statements and commercial distortions in this text. Be ruthless."

### Structure of an ideal research prompt

```
Role: You are [specific expert role] researching [topic].

Context: I need to understand [what exactly] for [what purpose].
My situation: [specifics - company size, market, restrictions].

Task: Research [specific question].

Limitations:
- Priority: academic/independent sources > practical experience > blogging/content marketing- Note the degree of confidence for each statement
- Share facts, opinions and advertising claims
- Explicitly indicate if information may be out of date
- Do not recommend specific commercial products, describe the criteria

Output format:
1. TL;DR (3-5 sentences)
2. Key findings (with confidence markings)
3. Alternative points of view/counterarguments
4. Blind spots and limitations of this study
5. Sources (with explanation of type: academic / practical / commercial)
6. What needs to be checked additionally
```

### Workflow LLM-research (step-by-step)

1. **Decomposition.** Break the question into sub-questions. Each sub-question is a separate request.
2. **First Pass.** Get basic answers. Don't believe them - these are hypotheses.
3. **Triangulation.** Verify key statements through web search and/or a second LLM.
4. **Adversarial check.** Ask the LLM to attack their own conclusions.
5. **Source verification.** Check key sources - do they exist? Are they quoted correctly?
6. **Synthesis.** Combine the results, noting the degree of confidence.
7. **Red team.** Final check for errors and distortions.
8. **Save.** Write the result into the knowledge base with links to sources.### Researcher biases (human factor)

The document above describes bias models. But the researcher has his own cognitive biases that add to the problem.

**Confirmation bias**
The researcher formulates the question in such a way that it already suggests the “correct” answer. “Why is Obsidian better than Notion?” instead of “Compare Obsidian and Notion for my tasks.” LLM willingly adapts to the direction of the question - sycophancy bias of the model + confirmation bias of the person = a vicious circle.

Compensation: formulate questions neutrally, start with open questions, consciously seek refutations of your hypothesis.

**Anchoring (anchor effect)**
The first answer received becomes the anchor. You evaluate all subsequent answers in relation to it. If the first model said “X is the best approach,” it is difficult to objectively evaluate the alternatives.

Compensation: start triangulation with different models at the same time, change the order of sub-questions between sessions, explicitly record: “My current anchor is X. What are the arguments for Y?”

**Framing effect**The way I posed the question was the answer I received. “What are the risks of approach X?” and “What are the benefits of approach X?” give completely different answers from the same model.

Compensation: ask one question in several frames, explicitly ask: “Describe both the pros and cons, and a neutral position.”

**Over-researching (research paralysis)**
Endless delving into details instead of jumping into action. Signs: new queries return what is already known, counterarguments are repeated, the level of confidence on key issues has stabilized. If three different sources say the same thing, you have reached saturation.

Compensation: before starting the research, determine: (1) what decision I am making, (2) what is the acceptable level of uncertainty, (3) the deadline for the research. When you reach any of the three, stop.

###Linguistic and cultural displacement

LLM training data consists of 60-70% English-language content. This results in a system bias:

- **Geographic bias**: the model by default thinks in US/EU market patterns. For the Russian, Asian, Latin American context, best practices may not work.
- **Cultural bias**: communication style, business norms, legal framework - everything is tied to the Anglo-Saxon tradition.- **Language bias**: in Russian, the model has access to less data and may give less accurate answers, especially for niche topics.

**Compensation:**
- Explicitly indicate geography and context: “for the Russian market / for a company in the CIS / for a Russian-speaking audience”
- Conduct some queries in English - this opens access to another layer of data
- Double-check: “Does this pattern work in my market, or is it a Western specificity?”
- For regulatory and legal issues - always verify through local sources

### Difference between "find out" and "decide"

Research for knowledge and research for solutions are different processes with different quality criteria.

**Research-Knowledge:** “How does Personal CRM work?” → wide overview, multiple points of view, maximum coverage.

**Research-Decision:** “Which Personal CRM should I choose?” → narrow focus on my limitations, clear criteria, quick action.

A typical mistake: starting with research-solution, but slipping into research-knowledge. Accumulating information is pleasant, making decisions in conditions of uncertainty is not.

**Compensation:**
- Before starting, determine the type: do I want to find out or decide?- To make a decision, formulate criteria BEFORE research, and not after
- Fix restrictions (budget, time, skills, ecosystem) - they cut off 80% of options
- Set a deadline: “After 2 hours of research, I make a decision with what I have”

### Multisession protocol

Serious research rarely fits into a single LLM session. We need a context transfer protocol.

**Problem:** Each new session starts from scratch. If you simply describe the result in words, nuances, reservations, and degrees of confidence are lost.

**Protocol:**
1. At the end of the session - extract the results into a structured format (extraction prompt, see examples in the CJM and Personal CRM projects)
2. Save to the knowledge base with links to sources
3. At the beginning of a new session, let the model read the saved document as context
4. Explicitly state: “These are the results of a previous study. Don’t repeat them, continue from this point.”
5. Synthesis periodically: combine results from several sessions into one document

**Anti-pattern:** retell the results of the last session in your own words. This introduces the narrator's own bias and loses detail.

### LLM research quality checklist

Before finalizing the results, check:- [ ] Are there any links to primary sources (not just blogs)?
- [ ] Are alternative points of view presented?
- [ ] Low confidence statements marked?
- [ ] Have key facts been verified via web search?
- [ ] Potential commercial biases identified?
- [ ] Vocal minority vs silent majority taken into account?
- [ ] Tested for survivorship bias (are there examples of failures)?
- [ ] Is the result relevant to MY situation (and not generic best practice)?
- [ ] Is the information relevant for the current date?
- [ ] Are the limitations and blind spots of the study indicated?
- [ ] Has your own confirmation bias been checked (neutral wording of questions)?
- [ ] Language/cultural bias taken into account (relevant to my market)?
- [ ] Is the type of research (knowledge vs decision) and stopping criteria defined?
- [ ] Are the results saved in the knowledge base for multi-session use?
- [ ] Has research slipped into over-researching (data saturation)?
